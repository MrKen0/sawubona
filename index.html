A story you might recognise

The call you dread making.

"I call every Sunday. But every Sunday I hang up feeling like it wasn't enough."

You came to this country with your father's voice in your head. Work hard. Make something of yourself. Honour what he sacrificed so you could be here. And you did. You have a career, a house, children of your own. You've built exactly what he wanted for you.

But somewhere along the way, your father got older. And now Sunday mornings end with a phone call that leaves you sitting quietly for a while after you put it down. He sounds fine. He always sounds fine. That's the problem.

He came to England in 1981. He worked for forty years — long shifts, early mornings, always the first in, last out. He built his life here the Ghanaian way: quietly, diligently, without complaint. Now he's retired. The routine that defined him is gone. His hometown association meets once a month. Church on Sundays. The rest of the week, the flat is very quiet.

"He used to have opinions about everything. Now he just says he's managing. That word — managing — is what keeps me up at night."

He watches GTV on his tablet. He still makes kelewele on a Friday, the whole flat smelling exactly like Kumasi in 1975. He has his faith, his few old friends. But they're scattered now — some moved back home, some in care homes, some gone. The men from that era don't talk about loneliness. They manage.

You've looked at options. Care agencies feel wrong — he doesn't need care, and he'd find it insulting if you suggested it. What he needs is someone who speaks Twi without switching to English halfway through. Someone who remembers what Ghana was before everything changed. Someone who can sit with him and have a real conversation — not an appointment.

That's what Sawubona is. Not a care service. Not a clinical solution. A real person from his world — who shows up at his door, twice a week, and simply spends time with him. So he has something to tell you about when you call on Sunday. So that word — managing — becomes something better.

You're not failing him. You're here. And now, so is Sawubona.

Sound familiar?

For companions

You built a life here. Now give something back to it.

"I retired and nobody told me retirement could feel this empty."

You gave this country forty years. Maybe the NHS. Maybe Transport for London. Maybe the council, or a factory floor, or a shop you ran yourself. You got up before dawn. You were reliable. You didn't complain. That's how it was done — that's how your father did it in Kumasi, and that's how you did it in London.

Then retirement came. And with it, a silence nobody prepared you for. Your children are busy with their own lives — as they should be. Your old workmates have scattered. The hometown association meets once a month. Church on Sundays. But the days in between have a weight to them that you don't quite have words for.

You're not unwell. You're not helpless. You're a man who built something — and you're not finished yet. But the world has a way of making Ghanaian men of a certain age feel like they've become background. Like they're waiting for something, but nobody's told them what.

"I go to visit Mr Asante on Wednesdays. We talk about Kumasi in the old days, about football, about everything. I drive home and I feel like myself again."

Sawubona companions are men exactly like you. Ghanaian, recently retired, with a lifetime of culture and perspective and presence stored up and nowhere to put it. The name says it all — we see you. And the elders you visit need someone who sees them too. What we're asking is simple: give two afternoons a week to a man in your community who needs exactly what you have.

You'll be paid. You'll be trained and supported. You'll have the full backing of a structured, professional organisation behind you — the DBS check, the safety protocols, the guidance. But what you're actually bringing can't be trained into someone who doesn't already have it. The Twi. The cultural memory. The shared knowledge of what it means to be Ghanaian in England for forty years.

This is not a gig. This is not pocket money. This is purposeful, dignified work that your community genuinely needs — delivered by someone it trusts, because you are part of it. We're not recruiting workers. We're recognising men who have something extraordinary still to give.

If that sounds like you, we'd like to meet you.

Before you register, a few things to know:

Minimum 2 visits per week

Each visit is 2 hours. We need companions who can commit to a regular weekly schedule with the same elder. Consistency is what builds trust and is central to how Sawubona works.

Nigerian or Ghanaian heritage

Our service is built on cultural and linguistic authenticity. We match companions who genuinely share the elder's background — this is the foundation of what we offer.

Enhanced DBS check required

We support you through the entire process and cover the cost. This protects the elders in our care and gives families confidence. There are no shortcuts here.

Self-employed, flexible schedule

You work as an independent companion — you set your own days and times within what your elder needs. You'll be paid per visit, with full transparency on rates.

Short online training required

Before your first visit, you complete our induction covering safeguarding awareness, lone worker safety, and the Sawubona code of conduct. Delivered by us, approximately two hours. This is a requirement, not optional.

Based in or near our launch areas

We're launching in London (Peckham, Lewisham, Barnet, Harrow, Enfield) and Hertfordshire. If you're outside these areas, register anyway — we're expanding.

About Sawubona

Sawubona. Connection, not care.

Sawubona is not a domiciliary care agency. We don't provide personal care, medical support, or clinical services. What we provide is something harder to find and harder to name: a familiar face, a shared language, and a conversation that feels like home. In Zulu, sawubona means 'we see you' — the full you, your history, your language, your story. For Ghanaian elders, that means someone who speaks Twi, who remembers what Ghana was, and who sits with them as an equal. That is what every visit delivers.

What we are

  • A companionship-only subscription service
  • Culturally and linguistically matched visits
  • In-home — your elder's home, always
  • Visit update after every completed visit
  • Simple 30-day cancellation notice
  • Focused on Nigerian and Ghanaian communities first

What we are not

  • Not a care agency or healthcare provider
  • Not CQC-regulated (no personal care given)
  • Not tied to a physical premises
  • Not a one-size-fits-all generic service
  • Not replacing your family's role
  • Not complicated — just human
The process

Simple from day one.

01

You tell us about your elder

Language preference (Yoruba, Igbo, Twi, Ga, English), personality, hobbies, and what kind of companionship would mean most to them. We listen carefully — the match depends on it.

02

We find the right match

We pair them with a DBS-checked companion who shares their language and cultural background. We shortlist and present options — you choose. Because this relationship has to feel right.

03

Visits begin at home

Your companion visits regularly — conversation, a walk, shared stories, a card game. No clinical agenda. Just real presence, at their pace, in their space. The same companion, every time.

04

You stay in the loop

After every visit, you receive a brief update — how she was, what they talked about, and how the relationship is growing. So you always know, without having to ask.
