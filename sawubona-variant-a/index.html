A story you might recognise

The call you dread making.

"I call every day. But I can't be there every day."

You came to this country with a plan. Work hard. Build a life. Give your children everything your parents gave you — and more. And for a long time, it worked. You have a career, a mortgage, children of your own. A life you're proud of.

But somewhere in the middle of all of it, your mother got older. And your father. And now there's a phone call every evening that leaves you with a feeling you can't quite name. Not worry exactly. Guilt, maybe. Or grief — but for someone who's still here.

She doesn't complain. That's the thing. She never has. She just says "I'm fine, don't worry about me" — in Yoruba, in Twi, in the language that still feels like home to her even though she's been in this country for thirty years. She watches Nigerian TV series. She cooks the same jollof every Sunday. She has a church she loves. But the rest of the week stretches out in ways she won't tell you about.

"She asks the same questions twice now. Not because she's forgotten — because there's no one to talk to."

The hardest part isn't the distance. It's knowing that the thing your mother needs — real conversation, someone who gets the references, who doesn't look confused when she mentions home — is the one thing you can't fully provide from three tube stops away, working full time, raising your own family.

You've looked at care agencies. But she doesn't need care. She's sharp. She's independent. She just needs someone to sit with. Someone who can talk to her about the old neighbourhoods, who knows what chin chin tastes like at Christmas, who won't make her feel like a patient.

That's what Sawubona is. Not a care service. Not a clinical solution. A real person — someone from her world — who shows up at her door, twice a week, and simply spends time with her. So she has something to tell you about when you call. So you can stop dreading that you're not doing enough.

You're not failing her. You're here. And now, so is Sawubona.

Sound familiar?

Thirty years of working. Now, something that matters.

"I retired and felt invisible. Like the world had moved on without me."

You spent decades being useful. Maybe you worked in the NHS, or a school, or a bank, or you ran your own business. You raised children, you kept a household going, you were the person others came to when they needed something. Your whole life, you were somebody.

Then retirement arrived. And with it, something unexpected — a kind of quiet that nobody warned you about. Your children are busy. Your friends are scattered. The church group meets on Thursdays. The rest of the week, time moves differently than it used to.

You're not in poor health. You're not struggling. But you're also not quite sure what you're for anymore — and that's a harder thing to admit than any of us like.

"I sit with her on Tuesday afternoons. We talk about Lagos in the 70s. I go home feeling like I did something real."

Sawubona companions are people exactly like you. Nigerian, Ghanaian, recently retired, with a lifetime of culture and warmth and experience stored up and nowhere to put it. The name says it all — we see you. And the elders you visit need someone who sees them too. What we're asking is simple: give two afternoons a week to an elder in your community who needs exactly what you have.

You'll be paid. You'll be trained and supported. You'll have the full backing of a structured, professional organisation behind you — the DBS check, the safety protocols, the guidance. But the thing you're actually bringing can't be trained into someone who doesn't already have it. The language. The references. The shared memory of a place and a time and a way of being that means everything to the person you're sitting with.

This is not a gig. This is not something you do for pocket money between other things. This is purposeful work that your community needs, delivered by someone your community trusts — because you are part of it. We're not recruiting workers. We're recognising elders who have something extraordinary to give.

If that sounds like you, we'd like to meet you.

Before you register, a few things to know:

Minimum 2 visits per week

Each visit is 2 hours. We need companions who can commit to a regular weekly schedule with the same elder. Consistency is what builds trust and is central to how Sawubona works.

Nigerian or Ghanaian heritage

Our service is built on cultural and linguistic authenticity. We match companions who genuinely share the elder's background — this is the foundation of what we offer.

Enhanced DBS check required

We support you through the entire process and cover the cost. This protects the elders in our care and gives families confidence. There are no shortcuts here.

Self-employed, flexible schedule

You work as an independent companion — you set your own days and times within what your elder needs. You'll be paid per visit, with full transparency on rates.

Short online training required

Before your first visit, you complete our induction covering safeguarding awareness, lone worker safety, and the Sawubona code of conduct. Delivered by us, approximately two hours. This is a requirement, not optional.

Based in or near our launch areas

We're launching in London (Peckham, Lewisham, Barnet, Harrow, Enfield) and Hertfordshire. If you're outside these areas, register anyway — we're expanding.

About Sawubona

Sawubona. Connection, not care.

Sawubona is not a domiciliary care agency. We don't provide personal care, medical support, or clinical services. What we provide is something harder to find and harder to name: a familiar face, a shared language, and a conversation that feels like home. In Zulu, sawubona means 'we see you' — the full you, your history, your language, your story. That is what every visit delivers.

What we are

  • A companionship-only subscription service
  • Culturally and linguistically matched visits
  • In-home — your elder's home, always
  • Visit update after every completed visit
  • Simple 30-day cancellation notice
  • Focused on Nigerian and Ghanaian communities first

What we are not

  • Not a care agency or healthcare provider
  • Not CQC-regulated (no personal care given)
  • Not tied to a physical premises
  • Not a one-size-fits-all generic service
  • Not replacing your family's role
  • Not complicated — just human
The process

Simple from day one.

01

You tell us about your elder

Language preference (Yoruba, Igbo, Twi, Ga, English), personality, hobbies, and what kind of companionship would mean most to them. We listen carefully — the match depends on it.

02

We find the right match

We pair them with a DBS-checked companion who shares their language and cultural background. We shortlist and present options — you choose. Because this relationship has to feel right.

03

Visits begin at home

Your companion visits regularly — conversation, a walk, shared stories, a card game. No clinical agenda. Just real presence, at their pace, in their space. The same companion, every time.

04

You stay in the loop

After every visit, you receive a brief update — how she was, what they talked about, and how the relationship is growing. So you always know, without having to ask.
